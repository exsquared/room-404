The Craft Was Never About the Code

On AI, identity, and what makes an engineer irreplaceable

SD
Sahil Dhawan & Claude (Anthropic)
March 2026
10 min read

AI will not replace software engineers.

That's what I've been telling myself. And honestly, I'm not sure I believe it anymore.

I hope I'm wrong. I hope that in ten years we'll all look back at this and laugh. But right now, nobody's laughing. I'm still debugging memory leaks while AI is out there building full-stack applications from a paragraph of text. Or whatever you want to call it.

What bothers me the most? I now look at a piece of code and I have to ask myself: did someone actually build this, or did they just ask a machine to generate it?

The fact that I have to ask myself that. For the rest of my career. And I don't think I'm the only one.

But before I get into what I think about all of this, let me start somewhere else first.

• • •

For the first time in my career, I feel like the ground underneath me is moving in a direction I can't fully control. And I'll be honest with you — even after two decades of building software, even after surviving multiple recessions, the dot-com crash, and every industry upheaval in between — I feel insecure. And not the kind of insecurity where you question your last pull request. The kind where you lie awake wondering if the thing you've spent your entire adult life getting good at might just... not matter anymore. At least not in the way it used to.

But I'm not writing this to add more fear to a room that already has plenty. I think we've all had enough of that. I'm writing this because I've spent the last year watching, thinking, and trying to understand — and I think there are things worth saying. Especially to people like us. People who build things.

Why We Fell in Love With This

Let me take you back for a moment.

Do you remember why you got into this? Not the job. Not the salary. The thing that got you.

For me, it was a random weekend in New York City, back in 2006. I was working at Thomson Reuters on a product called Case Law — a research tool for law firms. I was out with a friend, and there was a guy with us I'd never met. We started chatting, and he mentioned he was a lawyer. I casually asked if he'd ever used Case Law.

He looked at me like I'd asked if he'd ever used oxygen. "Hell yes. It's like Google for us. Every lawyer uses it."

I told him I was on the team that built it. He just stared at me. "For real? Man, that is awesome. You guys are making our life so easy."

That moment. Standing on a sidewalk in New York, hearing a complete stranger tell you that the thing you helped build — the thing you stayed late debugging, the thing you argued about in code reviews, the thing that sometimes felt like it would never ship — was making a real difference in someone's life. That it mattered.

I was hooked. Not on code. On that feeling.

It was never about writing code. Code was just the medium. What I actually fell in love with was thinking. The act of taking something messy and ambiguous, breaking it down, understanding the constraints, and building something that works. Something elegant. Something that solves a real problem for a real person.

You can't shortcut that kind of understanding. It comes from years of getting things wrong, learning why, and getting them right the next time. Every shortcut you didn't take, every difficult problem you sat with instead of walking away from — that's what made you who you are as an engineer.

The work is the point.

“
कर्मण्येवाधिकारस्ते मा फलेषु कदाचन
You have the right to your labor, but not to the fruits of your labor.
— Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2, Verse 47

Three thousand years before anyone worried about AI, the Gita had the answer. The work is the point. Not the output. Not the reward. The act of doing it with everything you have.

What AI Changes

Here's where it gets uncomfortable.

With AI, you can skip all of that. You can generate a working application without understanding a single line of what's underneath. No struggle. No debugging journey. No deep understanding earned through failure. You describe what you want, and it appears.

I've watched people with no engineering background build in minutes what used to take experienced teams days. And some of it is genuinely good. That's the part that's hard to say out loud after you've spent your whole career earning your craft the slow way. That feeling I talked about — the one from the New York sidewalk? AI doesn't need it. AI doesn't even know it exists.

There's a term people use now — vibe coding. You talk to the machine, it writes the code, you look at the output, you adjust your prompt, and you keep going until it works. No one reads the code. No one needs to understand it. It just... works. Usually. For now.

I'd been hearing for years that AI can write code. But I only truly started using it two months ago, when I had to build a proof of concept with a timeline that was, frankly, insane. It wouldn't have been possible without LLM support. And once I started, I couldn't stop.

There were days I had AI agents writing code for me while I was driving. I'd glance over and watch what the LLM was thinking, what it was building, and only step in when it started going in the wrong direction. I've become less of a writer and more of an observer — a supervisor of machines that build things. And that shift happened so gradually I almost didn't notice it.

But There's a Catch

Here's what nobody shows you in the demos.

// Prompt: "check if user is old enough to sign up"
// What a human would write:
// const isEligible = user.age >= 18;

// What AI wrote:
const validateEligibility = pipe(
  extractUserMetadata,
  normalizeAgeField({
    timezone: Intl.DateTimeFormat().resolvedOptions().timeZone,
    calendar: 'iso8601',
    roundingMode: 'floor',
  }),
  applyComplianceRules(getRegionalConfig()),
  memoize({ ttl: 3600, key: 'eligibility' }),
  validateAgainstSchema(EligibilitySchema),
  tap(result => logger.info('Eligibility computed', result)),
);

// Works perfectly. Ships to production.
// Breaks 3 months later when someone changes
// getRegionalConfig() and nobody understands
// why sign-ups stopped working in Europe.
//
// The fix took 2 days. The original check
// would have been one line.

It works. It's clever. And when it breaks, nobody knows why.

I've seen this pattern over and over. AI agents don't just write clever code — they take shortcuts. They deviate from standards. They skip edge cases. They'll find the path of least resistance, just like we do. It turns out AI agents can be just as lazy as the humans who built them.

I still don't trust them to write production-level code. Not fully. They'll over-engineer something that should be one line. They'll implement things the lazy way when you're not watching. They'll build something that passes every test but crumbles the moment a real user does something unexpected.

I'm not saying any of this to be bitter. I'm saying it because I think we need to be honest about what's happening before we can talk about what to do about it.

Why This Time Is Different

I've been through every wave. Desktop to web. The mobile explosion. Cloud migration. The SaaS era. Every single one of those shifts needed more engineers. They expanded the surface area of what software could do, and we were the ones who had to figure out how to build it.

This wave is different. It doesn't need more of us. It might need fewer.

Projects that used to require a team of ten can now be done by three people and a handful of AI tools. That's not a prediction — that's happening right now, in companies all around us. And the bar for "good enough" output is dropping fast.

Here's what I believe: AI will replace coders. People who write code for a living — just code, nothing else — are in real trouble. But engineers? The ones who think through architecture, wrestle with edge cases, and carry years of production scars in their head? I don't think a machine replaces that. Not yet. The question is which one you are.

That's not something I say lightly, and I'm not saying it to scare anyone — I think we've all scared ourselves enough already. I'm saying it because pretending otherwise doesn't help anyone.

And if you're feeling scared right now — that's okay. Things are moving at a pace that nobody was prepared for. You're not the only one feeling this way. Not even close. But here's what I keep reminding myself: the process of learning new things hasn't changed. It's the same process you've used your entire career — the same curiosity, the same persistence, the same willingness to sit with something you don't understand until you do. The only difference is you have to do it faster now. But the process? That's still yours. Nobody automated that.

So let me tell you what I've been doing.

What I'm Doing About It

1

Expand Your Skill Tree

The era of "I'm a React developer" or "I'm a .NET backend engineer" is coming to an end. If your entire identity is tied to one framework, you're standing on a shrinking island. Learn systems design, product thinking, infrastructure. The more complete you become as a builder, the harder you are to replace — by AI or by anyone else. And if you already have a broad skill set, start sharing what you know. Teach others. Become the person people come to not just for code reviews, but for thinking.

2

Know What AI Can and Cannot Do

Use the tools. Push them. Find the edges — because the edges are where your value lives. AI-generated code often works until it doesn't, and when it doesn't, nobody understands why. Who's responsible when AI-generated code breaks in production at 2 AM? Who debugs it? If you understand the tools deeply enough to answer these questions honestly, you become the most valuable person in the room.

3

Use AI as a Tool, Not a Replacement

I don't want AI to architect my systems. But I absolutely want it helping me move faster on boilerplate, research, exploration, and test scaffolding. I'll be honest — after two months of working this way, I don't want to go back to writing basic traditional code. And that's okay. AI can touch everything around my work. But the work itself — the thinking, the trade-offs, the decisions — that's still me. That's still us. You're still the one driving. You're just driving a faster car.

4

Lean Into the Problems AI Can't Solve

Think about the hardest project you've ever worked on. Not the one with the most lines of code — the one that was the most confusing. The legacy system nobody understood. The client who said one thing and meant another. No AI could have figured that out — because the problem was too human. The messier the problem, the safer you are.

5

Hold On to Your Fundamentals

When everyone can generate code, the person who can explain why it's wrong becomes the one everyone needs. The architecture. The design patterns. Spotting the edge case nobody else sees. Knowing that this approach will break under load because you watched something similar fall apart in production seven years ago. That's not code — that's judgment. And judgment is earned, not generated. Hold on to it. It might be worth more in five years than it is today.

Those are the practical things. But I want to go deeper for a moment. Because there's something about this conversation that goes beyond tips and career advice. Something about what it actually means to be great at what you do — and why that's never been about the tools you use.

“
There is a crack in everything. That's how the light gets in.
— Leonard Cohen

What Greatness Looks Like Now

If you're an engineer reading this and you're feeling overwhelmed — feeling like the thing you worked so hard to get good at is slipping away from you — I want you to know that I feel it too. I think every honest engineer does right now. And I think that feeling, as uncomfortable as it is, is actually proof that you care about this craft. People who don't care don't feel this way.

Satyajit Ray pawned his wife's jewelry to finance Pather Panchali. He shot on weekends because he couldn't afford to quit his day job. It took him three years to finish. Studios rejected it. Then it won Cannes and changed Indian cinema forever. No algorithm would have funded that film. No AI would have waited three years.

Greatness has never come from taking the easy path. It comes from the struggle, the doubt, the three years of shooting on weekends with pawned jewelry. It comes from fingerprints — the particular way a human sees a problem that no one else sees the same way. When everything is generated, nothing has fingerprints. When everything looks the same, nothing stands out. Brands will figure this out. Companies will figure this out. And when they do, they'll come looking for the people who can make things that feel human.

I think the biggest flex you can have in five years as an engineer is being someone who can actually build things and explain why they work. Not someone who can prompt their way to a demo. Someone who made hard trade-offs intentionally. Who can look at an AI-generated system and say, "This is wrong, and here's why — and here's what we should do instead."

That requires something AI can't give you. It requires character. It requires the scars from production incidents and failed projects and systems that fell over at scale. It requires intuition you can't download — the kind you have to earn.

The Real Work

Remember that proof of concept I mentioned — the one with the insane timeline? Here's what actually made it work. It wasn't the AI. It was the weeks before I ever opened a terminal.

I went to the customer. I sat with them. I spent time understanding their process — how they actually worked, not how they said they worked. I asked questions. A lot of questions. Sometimes the same questions multiple times, because the answer changed when they thought about it harder. I did my own research. I mapped out edge cases they hadn't thought of yet. I built a mental model of what this system needed to be before a single line of code existed.

And then I let the AI build it. With me guiding every decision, every architecture choice, every trade-off. The AI wrote the code. But I wrote the thinking.

When we showed it to the CIO, he was blown away. He said it was way more than a POC — it felt like a real product. And on the surface, it did. But I knew the truth. A proof of concept that wows a room is not a product that survives production. It doesn't have the error handling for the edge case that happens once a month. It doesn't have the security audit, the monitoring, the documentation. Building the real thing still takes everything it always took — architecture, discipline, a developer who cares about the details nobody sees. AI made us faster. It didn't make us unnecessary.

If I had just asked an LLM to "build me a care management platform" and walked away? It would have been a disaster. A beautiful, functional, completely wrong disaster.

No AI is going to replicate that feeling. The feeling of sitting with a customer long enough to understand what they actually need. Of earning their trust. Of building something that makes them say "this is exactly what we needed." Because that feeling doesn't come from the output. It comes from the work that happened before the first line of code.

Everything has to cost something in order to be great. You can't have greatness in abundance. That's not how this works.

We didn't get into this to take shortcuts.

We got into this for that feeling. The one you got on a sidewalk in New York when a stranger told you your work mattered. The one you got when a CIO looked at your POC and couldn't believe what he was seeing. The one no machine can generate, because it only comes from doing something hard and knowing it was you.

That hasn't changed. And I don't think it ever will.

Yes, I used AI to help write an article about not letting AI replace us. I see the irony.
